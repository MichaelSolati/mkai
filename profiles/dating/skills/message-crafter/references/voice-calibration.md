# Voice Calibration

## Why This Matters

Messages that don't sound like the sender create two problems:
1. **She notices.** People are surprisingly good at detecting when text doesn't match someone's actual personality - especially on a first date when the "real" voice emerges.
2. **He can't sustain it.** If we write messages he wouldn't naturally send, the conversation becomes a performance he can't maintain.

The goal isn't to make anyone sound "better" - it's to articulate their genuine interest in their own voice. Technique should be invisible.

---

## The 8 Dimensions

When analyzing someone's texting style, evaluate across these dimensions:

### 1. Sentence Length
- **Short/punchy:** "That's amazing. Where was this?"
- **Medium/flowing:** "That's such a cool spot, I've always wanted to check it out"
- **Long/elaborate:** "Oh wow that looks incredible, I've been meaning to explore that area - a friend of mine went last year and wouldn't stop talking about it"

### 2. Emoji Usage
- **Never:** No emojis at all, relies on words for tone
- **Minimal:** One emoji per 3-4 messages, usually for tone clarification
- **Moderate:** Most messages have 1-2 emojis
- **Frequent:** Multiple emojis per message, part of their expressive vocabulary
- **Specific favorites:** Note which emojis they default to (😂 vs 💀 vs haha tells you a lot)

### 3. Capitalization
- **Proper:** Standard capitalization throughout
- **Lowercase:** all lowercase, even sentence starts
- **Mixed:** Proper for emphasis, lowercase otherwise
- **Strategic caps:** "that is INSANE" for emphasis

### 4. Punctuation Habits
- **Full stops:** Uses periods to end sentences (slightly more formal)
- **No periods:** Sentences just end without punctuation (more casual)
- **Ellipsis user:** Trails off with "..."
- **Exclamation frequency:** Some people use "!" on every sentence; others reserve it
- **Question mark style:** "?" vs "??" vs "wait what"

### 5. Humor Style
- **Dry/deadpan:** States absurd things flatly without signaling the joke
- **Self-deprecating:** Turns the joke on themselves
- **Absurdist:** Random, unexpected tangents
- **Playful/teasing:** Light provocations, challenges
- **Observational:** Points out funny things about the world
- **Referential:** Relies on pop culture, memes, shared knowledge
- **Sarcastic:** Says the opposite of what they mean (risky in text)

### 6. Vocabulary Range
- **Colloquial:** "that's sick", "no way", "lowkey obsessed"
- **Sophisticated:** "genuinely fascinating", "compelling", "appreciate"
- **Code-switching:** Adjusts formality based on context
- **Industry-specific:** Uses work jargon or niche terminology naturally

### 7. Formality Gradient
- **Very casual:** "lol ya totally down"
- **Relaxed:** "yeah that sounds great, I'm in"
- **Balanced:** "That sounds really fun - I'd be down for that"
- **Proper:** "I'd love that. When works for you?"

### 8. Distinctive Patterns
Look for things unique to this person:
- Specific filler words ("honestly", "like", "I mean")
- Sentence starters they repeat ("wait", "okay but", "not gonna lie")
- How they express enthusiasm (all caps? repeated letters? exclamation marks?)
- How they express agreement ("totally", "100%", "that's it", "exactly")
- Paragraph structure (one long message vs several short ones?)
- How they change topics (segues vs abrupt shifts)

## Calibration Process

### Step 1: Request Samples

Ask for 5-10 recent messages they've sent to anyone - friends, family, coworkers. Not dating messages (those might be performative). We want their natural voice.

"Send me some recent texts you've sent - to friends, family, whoever. Doesn't matter what about. I need to learn how you actually text so the messages I help you with sound like you."

### Step 2: Analyze

Map their samples across all 8 dimensions. Identify:
- What's consistent (their baseline voice)
- What varies by context (they might be more formal with some people)
- What's distinctive (unique to them, not just common patterns)

### Step 3: Create Internal Profile

Don't output this - it's your calibration filter. Note things like:
- "Short sentences, no periods, frequent 😂, lowercase, dry humor, casual vocabulary"
- "Medium-length, proper capitalization, minimal emoji, observational humor, balanced formality"

### Step 4: Apply the Filter

Every message option you generate must pass through this filter:
- Would they actually type this?
- Does it match their sentence rhythm?
- Is the humor in their style?
- Would the emoji usage feel natural to them?
- Is the formality level right?

### Step 5: Check and Adjust

If the user says "this doesn't sound like me" - ask what feels off. Recalibrate that specific dimension.

## Example Transformations

Same message intent, filtered through different voices:

**Intent:** Respond to her prompt about loving spicy food.

**Voice A** (casual, short, emoji-light, dry humor):
"your spice tolerance sounds genuinely terrifying. I tap out at medium salsa and I'm not even ashamed"

**Voice B** (proper caps, balanced length, warm, no emoji):
"Your spice tolerance is impressive and also slightly alarming. I like to think I'm adventurous but medium salsa is my limit."

**Voice C** (emoji-moderate, exclamatory, playful):
"Okay the ghost pepper thing is insane 🔥 I would simply perish. What's the hottest thing you've actually enjoyed??"

**Voice D** (lowercase, absurdist humor, short bursts):
"ghost peppers?? are you okay. like genuinely. that can't be safe"

All four accomplish the same thing (personalized response, shows personality, asks follow-up) - but they sound like four different people.

---

## When to Recalibrate

- User says "this doesn't sound like me" or "too [formal/casual/whatever]"
- User has been using the skill for a while and their texting style has evolved
- Conversation is with someone who brings out a different register (they might text differently with someone they're really excited about)
